Methodology & Data Standards

Inclusion criteria: Events are included where there is at least one credible open-source report — an official Ukrainian military or intelligence statement, verified video evidence, or reporting by two or more independent outlets. Russian acknowledgement is noted where it exists but is not required for inclusion.

Damage classification: A consistent taxonomy is applied across all entries. Sunk = total loss, vessel confirmed on seabed. Destroyed = total loss confirmed on land or in air. Damaged = partial damage, target operational or repairable. Shot Down = aerial asset destroyed in flight. Disrupted = infrastructure or operation temporarily put out of action.

Personnel casualty figures: This tracker uses Mediazona/BBC News Russian confirmed-by-name figures as its primary KIA metric — the most rigorous publicly available methodology, cross-checked against Russia's National Probate Registry. These figures are always a floor, not a ceiling. Statistical estimates from Meduza/Mediazona's probate registry model are cited alongside for context.

ABOUT THIS TRACKER
About

The Ukraine War Unmanned Systems Tracker is an independent open-source research project built and maintained by Andro Mathewson, PhD Candidate in War Studies at King's College London. It documents the confirmed and claimed combat use of unmanned systems — surface, ground, and air — in the Russian invasion of Ukraine.

The tracker is built from publicly available OSINT, official Ukrainian military releases, and verified reporting. All entries carry source citations for independent verification. It is maintained alongside ongoing PhD research and updated regularly.

This tracker is not affiliated with any government, military organisation, or commercial entity. It is not affiliated with any of the organisations listed as sources above. Links are provided for research and verification purposes only.

Original Analyses
ORIGINAL RESEARCH USING TRACKER DATA · PHD CANDIDATE IN WAR STUDIES · KING'S COLLEGE LONDON
The analyses below are original research conducted by Andro Mathewson, PhD Candidate in War Studies, King's College London, using data from this tracker and third-party sources. They represent working findings rather than peer-reviewed conclusions. Methodology notes are included with each analysis.
Weather and Shahed Interception Rates
PUBLISHED APRIL 2026 · AIR DEFENCE DATA: PETRO IVANIUK / UAF · WEATHER: OPEN-METEO HISTORICAL ARCHIVE API
n = 603 attack days

This analysis cross-references 603 Shahed/Geran attack days (Oct 2022–Mar 2026) from the Petro Ivaniuk air defence dataset with night-time weather conditions from the Open-Meteo Historical Archive API. Each attack day was classified as Clear, Overcast, or Adverse based on cloud cover and precipitation. The central question: does adverse weather degrade Ukraine's air defence performance, and does Russia exploit it?

WEATHER & INTERCEPTION — ORIGINAL ANALYSIS
Finding 1 · Interception rate by weather
~70%
Consistent across all weather conditions

Clear nights: 70.7% (n=110) · Overcast: 72.2% (n=193) · Adverse: 68.2% (n=300). The 4-point difference across conditions is not statistically significant. Ukraine's air defence performs consistently regardless of night-time weather.

Finding 2 · Cloud cover correlation
r = 0.051
p = 0.21 · Not significant

OLS regression of night-time cloud cover against Shahed interception rate shows a near-zero relationship. Cloud cover explains less than 0.3% of variance in intercept outcomes — weather is not a meaningful predictor of air defence performance.

Finding 3 · Russian launch timing
150 vs 125
Clear vs adverse weather launch volumes

Russia launches more Shaheds on clear nights (150.4/day) than on adverse nights (129.6/day). There is no evidence Russia times swarms to exploit poor weather conditions — refuting the intuitive assumption of weather-based operational timing.

Original analysis: Andro Mathewson, PhD Candidate in War Studies, King's College London · Air defence data: Petro Ivaniuk / Ukrainian Air Force · Weather data: Open-Meteo Historical Archive API ↗ · n=603 attack days · Shahed/Geran only · Oct 2022–Mar 2026
METHODOLOGY NOTE

Weather conditions were retrieved for Kyiv (50.45°N, 30.52°E) as a representative location. Night-time defined as 20:00–06:00 local time. Attack days with fewer than 10 Shaheds launched were excluded. Statistical test: OLS regression with Pearson correlation. Clear = cloud cover <30%, Overcast = 30–79%, Adverse = ≥80% or precipitation present. Data and code available on request.

Air Defence Saturation: Interception Rate vs Launch Volume
PUBLISHED APRIL 2026 · DATA: PETRO IVANIUK / UKRAINIAN AIR FORCE
n = 955 attack nights

This analysis tests whether Ukrainian air defence degrades under high launch volumes — identifying the saturation threshold above which interception rates fall. Attack nights are bucketed by total weapons launched and mean interception rates compared across nine volume bands.

Finding 1 · Degradation zone
61–150
weapons — degraded performance (66–67%)

Interception rate drops to 66–67% in the 61–150 weapon range — below the 74% overall mean. This is the only volume band that consistently degrades performance.

Finding 2 · High-volume resilience
83%
interception at 600+ weapons

At the highest attack volumes (600+, n=10), interception recovers to 83% — above the overall mean. Mass swarms do not overwhelm Ukrainian air defence in the way assumed.

Finding 3 · Non-linear relationship
74%
overall mean across all volumes

The relationship between volume and interception is non-linear. Medium-intensity attacks (61–150) are the most effective at degrading defences — not mass salvos.

METHODOLOGY NOTE

Volume buckets: 1–30 (n=285), 31–60 (n=153), 61–100 (n=166), 101–150 (n=153), 151–200 (n=98), 201–300 (n=36), 301–400 (n=18), 401–600 (n=36), 600+ (n=10). Colour classification: ≥70% = manageable (blue), 55–69% = degraded (orange), <55% = overwhelmed (red). No nights fell into the overwhelmed category.

Salvo Composition: Testing the 'Shahed as Decoy' Hypothesis
PUBLISHED APRIL 2026 · DATA: PETRO IVANIUK / UKRAINIAN AIR FORCE
n = 1,060 attack nights

A widely cited claim holds that Russia uses Shaheds as decoys to saturate Ukrainian air defence and enable missile penetration — the "Shahed as decoy" hypothesis. This analysis tests it directly: if true, mixed salvos (Shahed + missile) should produce lower overall interception rates than Shahed-only attacks.

Finding 1 · Hypothesis not supported
73% vs 75%
mixed vs Shahed-only interception rates

Mixed salvos (Shahed + missile) produce a 73% interception rate vs 75% for Shahed-only. The 2-point difference is within error margins. Mixed salvos do not measurably degrade overall interception.

Finding 2 · Missiles intercepted well alone
80%
interception rate on missile-only nights

Missile-only salvos (n=82) produce the highest interception rate at 80% — suggesting Ukraine's missile defence performs well independently of Shahed volume.

Finding 3 · Volume effect
113
mean weapons on mixed nights

Mixed nights use the highest volumes (113 weapons/night vs 101 Shahed-only). The marginal degradation in mixed salvos may reflect volume effects rather than a decoy function.

METHODOLOGY NOTE

Salvo types: Shahed Only (n=411), Mixed Shahed + Missile (n=497), Missile Only (n=82), Other/Unknown (n=70). Classification based on weapon type field in Petro Ivaniuk dataset. Interception rate = weapons destroyed / weapons launched per night. Error bars represent 95% confidence intervals.

Russian Weapon Mix Evolution: From Missiles to Shaheds
PUBLISHED APRIL 2026 · DATA: PETRO IVANIUK / UKRAINIAN AIR FORCE · AUG 2022–MAR 2026
Full campaign period

This analysis tracks the composition of Russian aerial strikes over the full campaign period — examining how the balance between Shaheds, missiles, and other UAVs has shifted since August 2022. Both absolute volume and proportional share are examined to distinguish scaling from substitution.

Finding 1 · Shahed dominance
85–90%
of launches by 2025–2026

Shaheds now constitute 85–90% of all Russian aerial weapons launched — up from roughly 45–50% in late 2022. The campaign has become a drone-dominant operation.

Finding 2 · Volume explosion
8,000+
weapons launched in peak months (early 2026)

Total monthly launch volume reached 8,000+ by early 2026 — roughly 20× the campaign's 2022 baseline. This represents both substitution away from expensive missiles and a raw expansion of strike capacity.

Finding 3 · Missiles declining as share
<10%
missile share by 2025–2026

Ballistic and cruise missiles have declined from ~40–60% of early campaign launches to under 10% proportionally — though absolute missile numbers remain significant. The strategic emphasis has clearly shifted to attrition via drone mass.

METHODOLOGY NOTE

Weapon types classified from the model field in the Petro Ivaniuk dataset using keyword matching: SHAHED (Shahed-136, Geran variants), MISSILE (Kalibr, Iskander, Kh-series, Kinzhal, Zircon), OTHER_UAV (non-Shahed drones), OTHER (unclassified). Monthly aggregation. Data: Aug 2022–Mar 2026.

Geographic Dispersion: Russia Spreading Attacks Wider Over Time
PUBLISHED APRIL 2026 · DATA: PETRO IVANIUK / UKRAINIAN AIR FORCE · JAN 2025–MAR 2026
2022–present

This analysis tracks how many Ukrainian oblasts are affected per attack over time — a measure of geographic dispersion. Higher values indicate Russia is spreading strikes across more regions simultaneously, which has implications for how Ukraine must allocate air defence resources.

Finding 1 · Widening footprint
3 → 8+
mean oblasts per attack, mid-2025 to Mar 2026

Mean oblasts affected per attack fell to ~3 in mid-2025 before accelerating sharply to 8+ by March 2026 — a near-tripling in geographic reach within nine months.

Finding 2 · Most targeted oblasts
Kharkiv
most attacked oblast (~128 events)

Kharkiv (~128 events) and Sumy (~115) lead by a significant margin — reflecting their proximity to the front line. Kyiv (~96) and Dnipropetrovsk (~98) follow as the primary strategic targets.

Finding 3 · Strategic implication
↑ Dispersal
stress on Ukrainian AD resource allocation

Wider geographic dispersion forces Ukraine to maintain air defence capacity across more regions simultaneously — potentially more effective than volume increases alone at degrading coverage.

METHODOLOGY NOTE

Geographic dispersion measured as mean number of distinct oblasts recorded as targets per attack day. Oblast data extracted from the regions field in the Petro Ivaniuk dataset. Rolling 3-month mean applied to reduce monthly variance. Oblast frequency chart covers full campaign period (Oct 2022–Mar 2026).
